# Via Occulta — World Story Bible

## Format Statement

**Via Occulta** is a **top-down / overhead tile-based 2D JRPG world** with overworld routes, towns, interiors, shrines, caves, ruins, and dungeons explored from a classic overhead tile-map view.

Combat-heavy scenes, especially in **Luciphilus**, may transition into a separate side-view or dedicated battle-screen presentation. The world itself remains primarily tile-based and overhead.

---

## Core Story Premise

The old roads have vanished from the maps.

For centuries, the kingdoms of the surface world survived by traveling the **Viae Occultae**: hidden sacred roads that passed through shrines, caves, drowned chapels, forest gates, sewers, moonlit ruins, and sealed underways. These roads were not merely paths. They were a living network of oaths, crystals, ritual locks, guild charters, and monster-bound disguises.

Now the shrines are failing.

The crystals no longer hold their light. Trade routes break without warning. Towns wake to find bridges replaced by fog, wells opening into catacombs, and chapel doors leading to places that should not exist. Monsters do not simply invade from the wilderness; they appear from inside the road-network itself, wearing the shapes of old local spirits, guild guardians, and forgotten dead.

The world is not ending all at once. It is being quietly rerouted.

To survive, three kinds of people become necessary:

- **Tenebrio** — the weak shriner who enters forbidden routes, maps what others cannot see, and survives through rites, disguise, and transformation.
- **Luciphilus** — the adventurer who clears dungeon threats, recovers relics, fights monsters, and confronts crystal-era conflicts.
- **Opiphilus** — the crafter and guild worker who repairs towns, restores workshops, builds tools, fulfills orders, and turns recovered fragments into usable civilization.

Together, they do not merely save the world by defeating a final boss. They rebuild the hidden infrastructure of the world one shrine, road, workshop, and guild project at a time.

---

## World Identity

Via Occulta is a 1-bit monochrome occult fantasy world.

Its tone should feel:

- ancient,
- ritualistic,
- lonely,
- guild-driven,
- dangerous but not hopeless,
- mysterious rather than purely grim,
- closer to a cursed pilgrimage than a standard heroic conquest.

The central fantasy is not “become the chosen one.”

The central fantasy is:

> **Find the hidden road, survive what guards it, bring back proof, and help the town remember how to use it.**

---

## The Three Linked Game Lines

### Tenebrio — The Shriner Exploration Game

**Role in the shared world:** discovery, mapping, shrine rites, hidden routes, transformation, disguise.

Tenebrio is about weakness, fear, stealth, ritual knowledge, and forbidden access.

The Tenebrio player is not a classic sword hero. They begin as a vulnerable human attached to shrines, chapels, pilgrim houses, or road-keeping orders. They survive by understanding local spirits and monsters well enough to pass among them.

Tenebrio contributes:

- map fragments,
- hidden route discoveries,
- shrine keys,
- disguise rites,
- monster-form knowledge,
- occult warnings,
- route unlocks,
- cursed relic identification.

Core fantasy:

> **You are weak, but you know the rites.**

### Luciphilus — The Adventurer RPG

**Role in the shared world:** combat, dungeon expeditions, boss threats, relic recovery, crystal conflict.

Luciphilus is the most traditional JRPG-facing game line. It follows adventurers, guards, monster hunters, relic seekers, and guild expedition parties who enter dangerous dungeons once Tenebrio has found or opened the way.

Luciphilus contributes:

- monster parts,
- boss relics,
- crystals,
- cleared routes,
- combat records,
- dungeon rewards,
- recovered weapons,
- proof that an area is safe enough for rebuilding.

Core fantasy:

> **You carry the light into the road after someone else has found it.**

### Opiphilus — The Crafter / Guild Game

**Role in the shared world:** crafting, repair, logistics, town interiors, ritual support, guild economy.

Opiphilus is about the people who make survival possible. Blacksmiths, herbalists, glassblowers, market workers, guild clerks, ritual assistants, and shrine-town residents convert dangerous discoveries into usable civilization.

Opiphilus contributes:

- weapons,
- armor,
- potions,
- lanterns,
- ritual glass,
- shrine repair kits,
- guild orders,
- town upgrades,
- workshop unlocks,
- market circulation.

Core fantasy:

> **You do not enter the abyss to conquer it. You make the tools that let the town endure it.**

---

## Shared-World Loop

The three game lines should reinforce one another.

1. **Tenebrio discovers** a hidden shrine route, forbidden door, monster disguise, or map fragment.
2. **Luciphilus clears** the combat threat inside the revealed dungeon, ruin, or cave.
3. **Opiphilus builds** the tools, repairs, recipes, charms, and guild infrastructure needed to hold the route open.
4. The world unlocks a new room, road, recipe, guild project, or story chapter.

Short version:

> **Tenebrio discovers. Luciphilus fights. Opiphilus builds.**

---

## Main Conflict

The Viae Occultae were once maintained by a compact between three powers:

1. **The Shrine Orders**, who knew the rites.
2. **The Guild Houses**, who maintained roads, workshops, and exchange.
3. **The Crystal Courts**, who enforced boundaries between the surface world and the hidden road-network.

That compact has failed.

No one agrees why.

Possible explanations inside the story:

- The crystals are dying.
- The guilds overused the hidden roads for profit.
- The shrine orders sealed a truth they should have preserved.
- The monsters are not invaders but displaced guardians.
- The moon or underworld is pulling the roads away from the surface.
- Someone is deliberately rewriting the map.

The truth should unfold slowly across all three game lines.

## The Hidden Road Network

The Viae Occultae are not ordinary roads.

They can appear as:

- chapel basements,
- forest glades,
- sewer gates,
- cave mouths,
- old shrines,
- moonlit bridges,
- market cellars,
- guild-hall doors,
- grave paths,
- ruined towers,
- drowned sanctuaries,
- monster dens.

A road can be opened by:

- a map fragment,
- a shrine rite,
- a crafted key,
- a monster disguise,
- a restored crystal,
- a guild charter,
- a boss relic,
- a repaired mechanism,
- a remembered name.

This gives the world a natural reason for maps, crafting, rituals, boss drops, and guild projects to matter.

## Factions

### The Shrine Orders

Keepers of old rites and route prayers. They preserve the symbolic logic of the hidden roads, but many of their rites are incomplete or corrupted.

Associated with:

- Tenebrio,
- chapels,
- monks,
- priests,
- shrines,
- transformation rites,
- forbidden maps.

### The Guild Houses

Practical organizations that keep towns alive. They control workshops, markets, expeditions, inventories, repair contracts, and road charters.

Associated with:

- Opiphilus,
- blacksmiths,
- herbalists,
- glassblowers,
- merchants,
- guild halls,
- crafting projects.

### The Lantern Companies

Adventuring companies that enter routes after they are discovered. They clear threats, retrieve relics, and confirm whether a route can be used safely.

Associated with:

- Luciphilus,
- swordsmen,
- dungeon expeditions,
- monster hunts,
- boss encounters,
- relic recovery.

### The Crystal Courts

Old political and magical authorities whose legitimacy came from controlling crystal boundaries. They claim to protect the world, but may have hidden the real cause of the collapse.

Associated with:

- old kingdoms,
- crystal law,
- sealed ruins,
- forbidden archives,
- court knights,
- lunar omens.

### The Road-Bound

Monsters, spirits, cursed citizens, transformed pilgrims, dead guardians, and other beings trapped inside the Viae Occultae.

They are not all evil. Some are hostile. Some are territorial. Some are former servants of the old road compact.

Associated with:

- disguises,
- transformation forms,
- local monster ecologies,
- cursed chapels,
- hidden contracts,
- lost names.

## The First Region: The Pilgrim Verge

The first playable region should be small and readable.

### Core locations

- **Greybell Shrine** — shared shrine-town hub.
- **The Broken Road** — exterior route leading away from town.
- **Mistfall Chapel** — cursed shrine/church ruin.
- **The Underwell** — sewer/cave entrance under the town.
- **The Guild Hearth** — workshop and guild-hall interior.
- **The Old Crystal Gate** — locked route to the next region.

### First story arc

The town of Greybell survives beside a shrine that no longer rings its road-bell. Without the bell, the hidden road network cannot be trusted. Caravans vanish, chapel doors open into wrong rooms, and monsters appear wearing the forms of local dead.

The player loop:

1. Tenebrio maps the changed route beneath Mistfall Chapel.
2. Luciphilus clears the Wraith of the Drowned Gate.
3. Opiphilus restores the Guild Hearth forge and repairs the shrine bell casing.
4. The town reopens the Old Crystal Gate.
5. The next region becomes reachable.

## Key Objects

### Map Fragments

Partial records of hidden roads. Some are drawn; some are sung; some are encoded as shrine geometry.

### Shrine Keys

Ritual objects that open roads only when used in the correct place, form, or phase.

### Road Charters

Guild documents that legally and magically authorize travel through certain restored roads.

### Crystal Shards

Fragments of old boundary crystals. They power repairs, spells, and locks, but may also attract road-bound monsters.

### Lantern Glass

Crafted glass used to make route lanterns. A major Opiphilus item category.

### Disguise Rites

Tenebrio rites that allow a weak human to pass as a local monster, spirit, or road-bound being.

## Visual Story Rules

Because the artwork direction is monochrome, the story should emphasize strong silhouette language.

Use:

- lanterns,
- bells,
- masks,
- shrine arches,
- road signs,
- black trees,
- white fog,
- moon circles,
- crystal shards,
- chapel windows,
- guild banners,
- workshop fire,
- monster outlines.

Avoid relying on color-coded lore. The world should read in black and white.

---

## Camera And Scene Rules

The main world should be:

> **Top-down / overhead tile-based 2D JRPG exploration.**

Use this for:

- overworld routes,
- towns,
- interiors,
- shrines,
- caves,
- chapels,
- ruins,
- guild halls,
- workshops,
- dungeons.

Battle scenes may switch to:

- side-view ATB,
- turn-based battle screen,
- or a dedicated combat room presentation.

The camera identity should never be described only as “top-down MMO.” The precise target is a classic tile-based 2D JRPG world.

## Initial Prototype Story Slice

### Title

**The Bell That Forgot the Road**

### Setup

Greybell Shrine has stopped ringing. The bell still swings, but no sound comes out. Since then, every mapped road beyond the town has become unreliable.

### Tenebrio task

Enter Mistfall Chapel and recover a map fragment from a room that only appears while disguised as one of the road-bound dead.

### Luciphilus task

Use the recovered map fragment to enter the chapel undercrypt and defeat the Wraith of the Drowned Gate.

### Opiphilus task

Use ghost silk, iron, lantern glass, and crystal dust to repair the bell housing and craft the first Road Lantern.

### Resolution

The bell rings once. The Broken Road becomes stable long enough to reveal the Old Crystal Gate.

---

## Guiding Summary

Via Occulta should not be framed as a generic MMO.

It should be framed as:

> **A 1-bit top-down tile-based occult JRPG shared world where hidden roads are discovered by shriners, cleared by adventurers, and rebuilt by guild crafters.**
